Forecasting US Retail Sales Using Time Series Models in R

Author

Sheneli Weerasinghe

Executive Summary

Retail businesses rely on accurate demand forecasts to plan inventory, staffing, and promotional activity. This project develops time-series forecasting models to predict monthly US clothing retail sales using data from the Federal Reserve Economic Data (FRED) database.

A logarithmic transformation was applied to stabilize variance, and seasonal differencing was used to address strong annual patterns in retail demand. Several ARIMA and ETS models were estimated and evaluated using out-of-sample forecast accuracy and residual diagnostics.

The ARIMA model provided the most reliable forecasts, outperforming ETS based on both accuracy metrics and Ljung–Box tests. The results highlight strong and predictable seasonal peaks in retail sales, particularly during the holiday period, which can support planning decisions related to inventory management, staffing, and promotional timing.

Differencing to Make Data Stationary

Training and Test Set

ret_tr <- retail_ts[1:385,]
ret_ts <- retail_ts[386:409,]

A test set was created using the last 24 months of data. The rest of the data was placed into a training set.

The data contains seasonality, therefore differencing is required to prevent biased forecasts. A unit root test determined that one seasonal difference needed to be added, which a lag of 12.

The Guerrero method suggested a Box-Cox transformation with λ ≈ 0.42. However, a logarithmic transformation (λ = 0) was used instead for simplicity and interpretability, as it effectively stabilizes variance and allows changes to be interpreted as approximate percentage growth.

Creating a Shortlist of ARIMA and ETS Models

ETS Models

Choosing Appropriate ETS Models

ETS_fit <- ret_tr |>
model(ETS = ETS(Sales), Drift = RW(Sales~drift()), NAIVE = NAIVE(Sales), SNAIVE = SNAIVE(Sales))

The ETS model automatically chosen was ETS(M,N,M). The benchmarks random walk, snaive and naive were chosen. Mean was not chosen as a benchmark due to seasonality and an increasing trend in the data. The other benchmarks were more appropriate.

Other potential models were considered. The first is the Damped Holt’s method, which may be more accurate due to dampening the effect of the trend.

Two more potential models that can be tested are the same Holt and Damped Holt models as above, but with additive errors.

So the short list of ETS models are: Holt(M,N,M) Damped Holt(M,Ad,M) Holt(A,N,M) Damped Holt(A,Ad,M)

ETS_fit <- ret_tr |>
  model(
    Holt_MNM     = ETS(Sales ~ error("M") + trend("N") + season("M")),
    Damped_MAdM  = ETS(Sales ~ error("M") + trend("Ad", phi = 0.9) + season("M")),
    Holt_ANM     = ETS(Sales ~ error("A") + trend("N") + season("M")),
    Damped_AAdM  = ETS(Sales ~ error("A") + trend("Ad", phi = 0.9) + season("M"))
  )

ARIMA Model based on ACF

The ACF plot shows 7 significant lags before lag 12, therefore q = 7. The last significant seasonal lag is at 12, therefore Q = 1. So one potential model is ARIMA(0,0,7)(0,1,1)[12]

However, lag 6 & 7 are only slightly significant .Therefore q can also = 5 So another potential model is ARIMA(0,0,5)(0,1,1)[12]

ARIMA Model based on PACF

There are 6 significant lags before lag 12, therefore p = 6. The last significant seasonal lag is at 72 therefore P = 6. So the model based on PACF is ARIMA(6,0,0)(6,1,0)[12]. Lag 24 is barely significant, therefore P=2. So another potential model is ARIMA(6,0,0)(2,1,0)[12]

So 4 potential models are a1 = ARIMA(0,0,7)(0,1,1)[12], a2 = ARIMA(0,0,5)(0,1,1)[12], a3 = ARIMA(6,0,0)(6,1,0)[12] & a4 = ARIMA(6,0,0)(2,1,0)[12].

The innovation residuals for the ETS model look like they have a mean of 0. The residual plot shows a normal distribution. The ACF plot has patterns and has quite a few significant lags. This suggests that the residuals are auto-correlated. To further investigate if the residuals are white noise, the Ljung-Box test was used. The test produces a statistically significant p-value of 0, indicating significant auto-correlation.

For the ARIMA residuals, the innovation residual plot seems to have a mean of 0. The residual plot shows a normal distribution. The ACF plot also has patterns, and a few significant lags, meaning there is auto-correlation in the residuals.The the Ljung-Box test produces a p-value of 0.122, therefore the hypothesis that the residuals are not white noise is rejected.

Therefore the ARIMA(1,0,1)(0,1,1)[12] model is likely to produce the best forecast as it provided the lowest forecast error while maintaining well-behaved residuals. The ETS model fails to capture important temporal dependencies in the data and is therefore less likely to produce an accurate result.

Benefits and Limitations of the Models

Comparing ARIMA and ETS for this dataset

An advantage of ETS is that it can handles trends and seasonality directly. On the other hand, ARIMA assumes linear relationships and stationarity, meaning that for this dataset, the ARIMA models required differencing.

An advantage of ARIMA models are that they capture lagged effects, whereas ETS models do not. Capturing lagged effects is important when modelling retail turnover. For example, an economic shock that cause low sales in one month may cause sales to still be low in the next month.

Comparing ARIMA and ETS to other models for the dataset

ARIMA and ETS predict purely on temporal patterns - trends and seasonality. There may be other relationships in the data ARIMA and ETS are not capturing.

Unlike ARIMA and ETS, dynamic modelling allows for a change in parameters and can also adapt/switch behaviour. Therefore, dynamic modelling is better at capturing structural shocks such as COVID-19.

Final Model Selection

Based on AICc, out-of-sample forecast accuracy, and residual diagnostics, the ARIMA(1,0,1)(0,1,1)[12] model was selected as the final model.

This model achieved the lowest forecast error while maintaining residuals that resemble white noise, indicating that it adequately captures the underlying time-series structure.

This is likely because ARIMA explicitly models autocorrelation through autoregressive and moving average terms, allowing it to capture how shocks propagate over time. In contrast, ETS relies on smoothing components and does not fully account for serial dependence, which is reflected in the significant residual autocorrelation observed in the Ljung–Box test. Therefore, the ARIMA model appears to have capture the COVID shock more effectively than the ETS model.

Conclusion

This analysis developed and evaluated time-series forecasting models for monthly US clothing retail sales. Exploratory analysis revealed strong seasonal patterns and an upward trend, motivating the use of logarithmic transformation and seasonal differencing.

Among the models considered, ARIMA specifications provided the best overall performance. Residual diagnostics indicated that these models more effectively captured the underlying time-series structure compared to ETS, which exhibited significant remaining autocorrelation.

From a business perspective, the forecasts demonstrate consistent seasonal peaks in retail demand during late-year holiday periods. These insights can support operational decisions such as inventory planning, workforce allocation, and promotional scheduling.

Overall, the results highlight the importance of combining statistical modelling with domain understanding to produce forecasts that are both accurate and actionable.
